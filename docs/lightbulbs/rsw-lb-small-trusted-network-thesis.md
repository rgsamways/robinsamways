## The gap

Sparked by a real conversation Robin had with a lawyer friend: what marketplace do sophisticated white-collar legal services actually live in, given that his friend's clients come entirely by referral and word of mouth, not through any platform? Research into existing legal marketplaces (Avvo/Martindale-Avvo, etc.) showed they're built for high-volume, commoditized legal needs — personal injury, DUI, family law — the exact "ambulance chaser" segment being contrasted against. Nothing found serves the white-collar/high-stakes-referral segment, and the friend's own words explained why: *"you don't want a club everyone knows about."*

Generalizing past legal services specifically: the internet has built extremely well for two extremes — mass-scale marketplaces chasing network effects and visibility (Avvo's 25M+ monthly consumers), and one-off tools for individuals. It has largely ignored the middle: software for **small, closed, trust-based networks that deliberately don't want to scale or become visible**, because visibility and scale are exactly what would destroy their actual value (discretion, vetted trust, a referral meaning something specifically because of who made it).

## The idea

Not a product yet — a structural thesis: **build infrastructure for networks that want to stay small and trusted, without forcing them to either stay fully analog (word of mouth, no tooling at all) or accidentally become a public directory the moment any software touches them.** Software that helps something like a white-collar legal referral circle work better internally — track who trusts whom, capture context worth passing along with a referral, whatever the real friction points turn out to be — without ever becoming a searchable, visible-to-everyone platform, since that visibility is precisely the thing the network exists to avoid.

Farpost independently arrived at the identical underlying logic first: "it dies with the owner" is really a small-trusted-circle problem (the knowledge lives with one person or one tight local network because it never needed to scale past that), and Farpost's carrier-neutral "Switzerland" positioning is explicitly anti-mega-platform — it works *because* it isn't a big centralized directory. Recognizing the same pattern independently in a completely unrelated domain (white-collar legal referrals) is what turned this from "an interesting one-off observation" into "a thesis worth naming."
